- Infant mortality around the world -
The world has made a lot of progress in child survival over the past three decades. Despite this, an estimated 5.2 million children have under the age of five have died in 2019. With these statistics, more than half of these deaths are coming in Africa.

We will explore the potential causes for increases in infant mortality and what can be improved.

Basic water access

The World Health Organisation defines basic water access as having a protected drinking water source that takes less than thirty minutes to collect water from.

Africa by far has the lowest percentage access to basic water.

For instance, Central African Republic has the second lowest basic water access in the world (after Eritrea) at 37.20% and the highest infant mortality rate at 81 deaths.

Basic sanitation services

The World Health Organisation defines basic sanitation services as having access to a sewerage system or a composing toilet.

Once again, Africa has an extremely large number of countries with less than 50% with access to basic sanitation services.

Ethiopia, Eritrea and Chad all have the lowest percentage access to basic sanitation, and it shows! They also have an extremely high infant mortality rate!

Gross Domestic Product

Creating facilities to access safe drinking water, and creating infrastructure for sanitation services can be costly!

However, countries such as Cook Islands show that a high GDP is not required to have a low infant mortality rate as they only have 6.51 deaths but only have a GDP of 384 million!
